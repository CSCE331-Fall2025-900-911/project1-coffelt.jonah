Aristotelian Teleology

Aristotelian teleology is the philosophical view that all natural things have an inherent purpose or end, known as a telos. For Aristotle, understanding an object or a living being requires examining not only its material composition and form, but also its final cause—the goal toward which it naturally strives. For example, an acorn’s purpose is to become an oak tree, and its development can be best explained through this lens of final causality.

This concept of teleology deeply influenced Western philosophy and science for centuries. While modern science often avoids teleological explanations in favor of mechanistic ones, Aristotle’s framework remains significant in discussions of ethics, biology, and metaphysics. His notion that purpose is embedded in nature continues to shape debates about the meaning and order of the world.

Sources:

  • Shields, Christopher. Aristotle. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (2020)
